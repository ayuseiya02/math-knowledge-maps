1 Introduction

Over the last several years, there has been an explosion of interest in nearâ€“infrared spectroscopy. Improvements in infrared array detectors have led to the construction of sensitive, high resolution spectrographs for this wavelength regime (e.g. Hinkle et al. 1998; McLean et al. 1999). In addition, large area photometric sky surveys such as 2MASS (Skrutskie et al. 1997) and DENIS (Epchtein, 1997) have produced large target lists which require followâ€“up spectroscopy. In order to provide a comprehensive set of uniform high quality infrared stellar spectra, we undertook a multiâ€“wavelength survey of fundamental MK standards with the KPNO Mayall 4 m telescope utilizing the Fourier Transform Spectrograph. (1998; hereafter MEHS98) present the Hâ€“band data from our survey as well as outline a classification scheme based on several atomic and molecular indices. 5 Discussion and Summary

Because stars earlier than M5 (3000 K) have SEDs that peak at shorter wavelengths, features observed in the spectra of these stars over the Jâ€“band are intrinsically weak. However, there are several photospheric features present in this wavelength regime which are diagnostic and observing them in the Jâ€“band can help to penetrate large amounts of extinction. Absorption due to interstellar dust at the Jâ€“band is less than 1/313NUMBER:1 MULOP:divide NUMBER:31/3 that observed as visual wavelengths (e.g. Rieke and Lebofsky, 1985). For many objects it makes sense to obtain spectra at even longer wavelengths such as the Kâ€“band (WH97). However some targets surrounded by circumstellar material (such as young stellar objects or evolved stars) exhibit continuum excess emission due to thermal dust at temperatures as high at 1500â€“2000 K (e.g. Meyer et al. 1997). Because the emission from such hot dust peaks at 1.5â€“2.0 Î¼ğœ‡italic-mu\mum, it may be advantageous to observe these objects in the Jâ€“band. Perhaps the greatest utility of Jâ€“band spectroscopy (not demonstrated in this paper) lies in the classification of very cool stars (>RELOP:greater-than> M5), whose Iâ€“ and Jâ€“bands spectra are dominated by very broad atomic and molecular features (e.g. Kirkpatrick et al. 1993; Jones et al. 1994).

There are also technical reasons that make the Jâ€“band attractive: it can be observed with a warm spectrograph without any appreciable thermal background contributed by the optical train. However this requires a non-silicon based detector as standard CCDâ€™s are not efficient beyond 1.0 Î¼ğœ‡italic-mu\mum. Joyce et al. (1998b) report detectorâ€“limited performance using a HgCdTe infrared detector in a dewar blocked with a cold Jâ€“filter in the focal plane of an ambient temperature spectrograph while the Hâ€“band observations were still limited by thermal radiation from the spectrograph. Further the intensity of the OH airglow emission, an important source of noise in low resolution 1â€“2.5 Î¼ğœ‡italic-mu\mum spectroscopic observations, is roughly twice as strong in the Hâ€“band compared with the Jâ€“band (Maihara et al. 1993). However, in deciding whether or not to utilize the Jâ€“band spectral region for stellar classification, it must be remembered that telluric water vapor absorption can be difficult to treat over significant portions of the Jâ€“band spectral region.
